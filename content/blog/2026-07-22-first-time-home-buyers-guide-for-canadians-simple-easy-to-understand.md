Buying your first home in Canada can feel like everyone else got a handbook you never received. Deposits, pre-approval, the "stress test," insurance you didn't ask for — it's a lot of new words for one big decision.

This guide walks you through it in plain language: what a mortgage actually is, how much you really need saved, how approval works, and the programs that can help. No jargon, no judgment, no pressure — just a clear picture so your first move is a confident one.

## What a mortgage actually is

A mortgage is simply a loan to buy a home, using the home itself as security. You put in some money up front (your **down payment**) and borrow the rest from a lender. You then pay that back, plus interest, over time.

Two words worth knowing early:

* **Amortization**

   — the total time you take to pay off the whole loan (often 25 or 30 years).
* **Term**

   — the shorter stretch (very often five years) that your rate and conditions are locked in for. When a term ends, you 

  *renew*

   for the next one. (More on that in our 

  [mortgage renewal guide](http://localhost:3100/mortgage-renewal-canada-guide)

  .)

That's the whole shape of it. Everything below is just detail on top of this simple idea.

## How much you need for a down payment

Your down payment is the part you pay yourself. In Canada, the minimum depends on the purchase price ([Financial Consumer Agency of Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/financial-consumer-agency/services/mortgages/down-payment.html)):

* **5%**

   on the first $500,000 of the price
* **10%**

   on the portion between $500,000 and $1,500,000
* Homes priced 

  **over $1,500,000**

   require at least 

  **20%**

   down

So for a $600,000 home, the minimum is $25,000 (5% of the first $500,000) plus $10,000 (10% of the next $100,000) — **$35,000** in total.

Putting down **less than 20%** is completely normal for first-time buyers. It just means you'll need **mortgage default insurance** (from CMHC or another insurer), which protects the lender and lets you buy sooner with a smaller down payment. The premium is usually added to your mortgage rather than paid all at once. ([CMHC](https://www.cmhc-schl.gc.ca/consumers/home-buying/mortgage-loan-insurance-for-consumers/what-are-the-general-requirements-to-qualify-for-homeowner-mortgage-loan-insurance))

> **Good news for first-time buyers:** As of December 15, 2024, **30-year amortizations** are available to all first-time buyers (and to anyone buying a newly built home). Stretching payments over 30 rather than 25 years lowers your monthly payment, though you pay a bit more interest over the full life of the loan. ([Department of Finance Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/department-finance/news/2024/12/boldest-mortgage-reforms-in-decades-come-into-force-today.html))

## Getting pre-approved

A **pre-approval** is a lender's early read on how much you could borrow and at roughly what rate, based on your income, debts, and credit. It's not a guarantee, but it's genuinely useful:

* It tells you a realistic price range 

  *before*

   you fall for a listing.
* It shows sellers you're a serious buyer.
* It can hold a rate for a set window while you shop.

To prepare, it helps to have a sense of your income, your regular debt payments, and how much you've saved. You don't need it all perfectly organized to start a useful conversation — a rough snapshot is plenty.

## The "stress test," explained

Before approving you, lenders don't just check that you can afford today's rate — they check that you could still handle payments if rates were higher. That's the **stress test**.

You're qualified at the higher of your actual contract rate **plus 2%**, or a set minimum floor — a benchmark that has been **5.25%** in recent years. ([OSFI](https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/supervision/financial-institutions/banks/minimum-qualifying-rate-uninsured-mortgages)) These rules can change, so it's worth confirming what applies to your situation at the time you apply: `[Confirm current stress-test rate at time of publish — broker to confirm]`.

It sounds intimidating, but it's really a cushion in your favour: it's designed so a future rate change doesn't catch you off guard.

## Programs that can help first-time buyers

Canada has a few tools built specifically for people buying their first home. A broker can help you see which fit your situation:

* **First Home Savings Account (FHSA)**

   — save up to 

  **$8,000 per year**

  , to a 

  **$40,000 lifetime**

   maximum. Contributions are tax-deductible and qualifying withdrawals to buy your first home are tax-free. (

  [Canada Revenue Agency](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/individuals/topics/first-home-savings-account.html)

  )
* **Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)**

   — withdraw up to 

  **$60,000**

   from your RRSP toward a first home, to be repaid over time. (

  [Canada Revenue Agency](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/tax/individuals/topics/rrsps-related-plans/what-home-buyers-plan.html)

  )
* **Land transfer tax rebates**

   — several provinces and some cities offer first-time buyer rebates. These vary by location: 

  `[First-time buyer land-transfer rebates for our service area — broker to confirm]`

  .

You can often combine these — for example, saving in an FHSA *and* using the Home Buyers' Plan. The details matter, so it's worth a quick conversation before you commit to one path.

## What happens after you're approved

Once you've made an offer and it's accepted, the mortgage moves from "pre-approved" to a full application. Your lender confirms the property and your finances, and a lawyer or notary handles the closing paperwork.

From there you'll settle on the parts that shape your monthly life:

* **Rate type**

   — fixed (steady payments) or variable (can move with the Bank of Canada). There's no universally right answer; see our 

  [fixed vs. variable guide](http://localhost:3100/fixed-vs-variable-mortgage-canada)

  .
* **Payment schedule**

   — monthly, biweekly, or accelerated.
* **Closing costs**

   — legal fees, land transfer tax, and a few one-time expenses to budget for on top of your down payment.

## Common questions

**How much do I really need saved?** At least your minimum down payment (above), plus a bit for closing costs. You don't need 20% — many first-time buyers put down 5% with mortgage insurance.

**Does checking my options hurt my credit?** A single, well-timed pre-approval is very different from applying everywhere at once. A broker can help you compare without scattering applications.

**Is my income "enough"?** There's no single number — it depends on the price, your debts, and current rates. That's exactly what a pre-approval answers, without any commitment.

**What if my credit isn't perfect?** Very common, and not a dead end. There are paths for different situations — the point of a first conversation is to find yours, with no judgment.
